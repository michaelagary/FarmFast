Our Missions

Health Benefits
  • Freshly grown produce at farmers markets has more nutrients and vitamins and has spent less time being transported or stored from the time it was harvested.
  • Fruits and vegetables that are grown in season taste better.
Environmental and Social Impacts
  • The fast food industry encourages harmful practices like factory farming. Tightly packed animals produce manure that leaks into and contaminates water supplies. Mass farming also requires mass deforestation, which contributed 8-10% of the yearly carbon dioxide emissions between 2015-2017.1
  • Supporting local farmers helps keep them in business and provide eco-friendly, fresh produce to more people. According to the University of Nebraska-Lincoln, "On average, only 10 cents of each food dollar returns to the farmer", but "farmers who sell direct to consumers receive 80 cents of each food dollar".2 Local farmers produce less food waste, greenhouse gas emissions, and support the local economy.
  • Buying from local farmers helps people get more involved in the community.
Why should you care?
  • Climate change impacts everyone - increasing storms, wildfires, flooding, and water shortages and endangering wildfire
  • We are already seeing the consequences of climate change and it will only get worse unless people make eco-friendly changes in their lifestyle to limit overconsumption and waste.
How can you help?
  • Good News: there are small, easy changes everyone can make that have a significant impact!
    • Buy local produce through FarmFast
    • Use refillable waterbottles.
    • Take public transportation or bike a few times a week instead of driving.
    • Bring your own reuseable bags to the grocery store.
    • Go vegetarian or vegan just one day of the week.
    • Think ahead when ordering food or buying groceries to limit food waste.

Environmental Impacts
  • Food waste contributes 6-8% of yearly greenhouse gas emissions.3
  • Wasting food means wasting the water, time, and energy that went into growing, harvesting, and transporting that produce to grocery stores. In addition, the "estimated one-third of all the food produced in the world goes to waste" and ends up in the landfill, which releases large amounts of methane.3
